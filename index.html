Our letter

Dear University Presidents and Vice-Chancellors,

We are writing as a group of [XXX] academics who are deeply concerned by our universities’ collaboration with the fossil fuel industry. Universities across the United Kingdom and the United States currently accept vast sums in funding from fossil fuel companies for research aimed at solving the very problems this industry caused and continues to exacerbate. We believe this funding is antithetical to universities’ core academic and social values. It compromises their basic institutional integrity and, as a result, their ability to help address the climate emergency.

For these reasons, we are calling on U.K. and U.S. universities to institute a ban on accepting fossil fuel industry funding for climate change, environmental, and energy policy research.

Accepting fossil fuel industry funding for research meant to address the climate crisis undermines the academic integrity of such research. There is a clear parallel between accepting fossil fuel industry funding for climate change research and accepting tobacco industry funding for public health research. Already, numerous public health and research institutions reject tobacco money due to the industry’s extensive record of spreading disinformation around the public health consequences of its products. Today, the fossil fuel industry has employed disinformation tactics from the same playbook, working to sow doubt about climate science, silence industry critics, and stall climate action. How, then, can universities consider these companies appropriate partners for climate-related research?

Fossil fuel funding of climate change research produces a fundamental conflict of interest that compromises researchers’ academic freedom. Academics must be free to determine their own research agendas, speak their mind, and declare their findings without fear of censorship, reprisal, or the withdrawal of funding for future projects. That freedom is inevitably compromised by reliance on funding from an industry whose core business model is diametrically opposed to science-led climate action. Numerous studies also demonstrate that research funding by vested industry interests disproportionately yields results favorable to those interests, and that common safeguards like public disclosure of funding sources are often inadequate to mitigate this skew. We know that many of our colleagues who choose to accept fossil fuel funding strive to produce honest and independent research, often faced with few alternative funding pathways. However, the risk of skewed outcomes is endemic where research funding is dominated by companies with particular profit-driven agendas. In order to preserve the basic integrity of crucial climate-related research, universities therefore cannot continue to accept funding from companies whose business models turn on the maintenance of a fossil fuel economy.

Furthermore, accepting fossil fuel research funding fundamentally contradicts universities’ stated commitments to tackling the climate crisis. Fossil fuel companies have covered up and neglected the science of climate change for decades. Today, despite warnings from the world’s top energy organization that “no investment in new fossil fuel supply projects” can be made if the world is to limit global heating to 1.5°C, all fossil fuel majors continue to plan new extraction projects decades into the future. Shell is even fighting a Dutch court order to curb its emissions. Despite boasting about ‘ambitious’ sustainability plans, fossil fuel companies’ investments in oil and gas still dwarf their renewable energy investments, which represent less than 1% of their total capital expenditure. Even the investments they claim as ‘sustainable solutions’ are frequently used to facilitate additional extraction of fossil fuels and can produce more emissions than they prevent. Claims by fossil fuel companies that they are leaders in a green transition can therefore not be taken seriously, and so collaborating with these companies is inimical to any institution seeking to lead or just act responsibly on climate.

University research partnerships with fossil fuel companies play a key role in greenwashing their fossil reputations. When universities allow fossil fuel companies to buy connections to university research on key climate and energy issues, universities inadvertently sell these companies much-needed mainstream scientific legitimacy. We must not underestimate how valuable it is for fossil fuel companies to be able to report to policymakers, shareholders and the media that they are working with globally respected institutions on transition solutions. In short, through research partnerships with prestigious universities across the world, universities help to wash fossil fuel companies’ reputations green and distract from their records of climate destruction. Meanwhile, these companies’ behavior mars their academic partners’ credibility. This happens as governments hesitate to impose stricter sanctions on fossil fuel companies, continue to place their lobbyists at the heart of key climate policy decisions, and parrot their misleading scientific claims. Our planet cannot afford such denial and delay any longer.

Finally, there is a wider reputational risk for universities who seek to speak authoritatively on the climate emergency maintaining close ties to the fossil fuel industry. We are proud that many universities have publicly committed to tackling climate change as a top institutional priority. In particular, we are glad to see an emerging consensus that institutions of higher education must divest their endowments from fossil fuels. Yet in allowing fossil fuel companies to fund research, many of these universities display a vast inconsistency in their policies and espoused principles. Increasingly, fossil fuel industry sponsorship is undermining scientific institutions’ climate credibility, perhaps most notably in relation to the controversy over Shell’s sponsorship of a Science Museum exhibition on climate change. When universities have a pivotal role to play in global conversations about tackling the climate emergency, they cannot afford to have their voices compromised in this way, which is precisely what will happen if they continue to embrace the industry most responsible for climate breakdown.

We firmly believe that our universities and the research they produce are vital to delivering a rapid, just transition away from fossil fuels. However, such efforts are severely undermined by a dependence on fossil fuel industry funding. Academics should not be forced to choose between researching climate solutions and inadvertently aiding corporate greenwashing; our universities must provide an alternative. Wealthy, well-resourced universities in particular have a duty to lead the way in doing so. Fossil free research is vital for ensuring academics’ freedom to call out the fossil fuel industry’s disinformation campaigns, inadequate sustainability plans, and failure to accept basic scientific realities. To all universities, at this moment of extreme crisis, we urge you to heed our call and cut research ties with the fossil fuel industry.
